\section{Phase 6: Generative Moonshots}

With the Agent's policy successfully cloned, the objective of Phase 6 is to transition the Pienza framework from a single-agent study into a scale-invariant \textbf{Digital Twin} of Mexico City's ride-hailing marketplace. To achieve this, the project pursues three experimental deep-learning objectives designed to overcome the administrative and logistical bottlenecks of the physical world:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Quest to $O(1)$ Inference (NLP Transformers):} Real-time deployment of the \texttt{XGBoost} model is limited by the network latency and cost of external geocoding APIs. To bypass this, a locally-hosted \textit{Text-to-Zone Transformer} was architected to classify raw address strings directly into strategic macro-zones, enabling sub-10ms inference.
    
    \item \textbf{Generative Synthesis (Conditional GANs):} The limited ground-truth volume ($N \approx 4,700$) creates a data-sparsity constraint for robust market simulation. To resolve this, a \textbf{Conditional Generative Adversarial Network (cGAN)} was deployed to synthesize a 1,000,000-row manifold, effectively transforming the static dataset into an infinite \textit{Keras} generator.
    
    \item \textbf{Topological Routing (Graph-Network Analysis):} The synthesized manifold is utilized to execute Graph-Network analysis, mapping the economic connectivity between urban hubs and identifying the "path dependency" of high-yield mission sequences.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textit{Note: While this generative foundation sets the stage for advanced methodologies---including State-Space Models (SSMs), Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulations---these specific applications are designated as future research and are formally proposed for the \textbf{Pienza 2.0} roadmap.}

\subsection{The Quest to $O(1)$ Inference: NLP Transformers}
In high-frequency dispatch environments, inference latency is the primary constraint for the viability of a real-time decision agent. To satisfy the requirements of \textit{Online Transaction Processing} (OLTP), the project analyzed three generations of urban inference through the lens of algorithmic efficiency and system scalability.

The current (Phases 1-5) architecture relies on external geocoding APIs and Point-in-Polygon (PiP) algorithms, constrained by $O(N \times M)$ geometric complexity and high network I/O latency ($>500$ms). To bypass this bottleneck, the project engineered \textbf{miniBabel}: a locally-hosted Transformer that maps raw urban syntax directly to a \texttt{zone\_id}. By treating geographic resolution as a task of semantic pattern recognition, the system collapses the search space into constant-time $O(1)$ neural inference\footnote{While the internal complexity of the attention mechanism is quadratic to the sequence length $L$, the bounded nature of urban address strings transforms the inference into a fixed-cost operation relative to the number of strategic zones.}. This architecture reduces end-to-end latency to $<10$ms and provides a strategic bridge toward the high-availability horizon of bitwise H3 integer indexing (Table \ref{tab:babel_efficiency}).

\subsubsection{Linguistic Feature Engineering}
To transform high-entropy address strings into high-density neural signals, the project implemented a hierarchical preprocessing pipeline. This stage focused on strategic data augmentation to ensure the model generalizes across the Mexico City urban landscape:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Deterministic Normalization:} Collapsed thousands of syntactic variations (\textit{e.g., Av, Ave, Avenida}) into unified canonical tokens. This artificially increased signal density, allowing the subsequent neural architecture to focus on geographic disambiguation rather than basic syntactic learning.
    \item \textbf{Token Soldering:} Utilized high-precision Regular Expressions to fuse numeric and cardinal qualifiers with primary street identifiers (\textit{e.g., ``Eje 6 Sur'' $\to$ \texttt{eje\_6\_sur}}). This prevents the model from treating structural modifiers as isolated noise, anchoring them to their relevant spatial context.
    \item \textbf{Target Leakage Mitigation:} Deployed a ``Slash-Purge'' protocol to remove platform-generated neighborhood labels (\textit{e.g., ``Roma/Condesa''}) from the raw strings. By pruning these explicit identifiers, the model is forced to learn latent geographic features---such as street-level syntax and intersection patterns.
    \item \textbf{Zip Code Standardization:} Standardized all 5-digit postal codes by prepending a unique \texttt{cp} prefix (\textit{e.g., ``11590'' $\to$ \texttt{cp11590}}). This transformation isolates zip codes from other numerical data, creating high-precision spatial anchors for the neural engine.
    \item \textbf{Semantic Embedding (Word2Vec):} This Skip-gram architecture was used as a tool to explore spatial proximity within the urban lexicon. By encoding tokens into 64-dimensional vectors, the model internalizes the semantic relationship between adjacent zones.
\end{itemize}

A Token Lift analysis validated the preprocessing pipeline by identifying words with disproportionately high frequencies in specific zones relative to their global usage.

\subsubsection{Model Architectures: Custom vs. Pre-trained}
To identify the optimal mapping function between urban syntax and strategic zones, the project benchmarked a custom-built Transformer against a state-of-the-art pre-trained baseline.

\paragraph{Model A: miniBabel (Custom Urban Transformer)}
To prevent catastrophic overfitting on the boutique dataset, the \textbf{miniBabel} architecture was restricted to two attention layers and utilized a high-dropout bottleneck ($p=0.2$) prior to the classifier. To ensure high-value spatial anchors (\textit{e.g., specific street names}) were not diluted during processing, the model replaced standard Global Average Pooling with a \textbf{fused Max-Average pooling} mechanism. This allows the classifier to ingest both the holistic context of the address and its sharpest geometric signals simultaneously.

\paragraph{Model B: miniBETO (Spanish BERT Adaptation)}
For comparative benchmarking, the project adapted \textbf{BETO} (a pre-trained Spanish BERT model). Adapting a model trained on standard prose to condensed urban syntax required the replacement of the default classification head. To ensure a scientifically valid comparison, the modified miniBETO was equipped with the identical fused pooling and bottleneck constraints utilized in the custom miniBabel architecture.

\subsubsection{Optimization and Training Constraints}
Due to the inherent class imbalance of the \texttt{zone\_id} distribution, both models utilized the \textbf{Weighted F1-Score} as the primary evaluation criterion. Training was governed by a multi-layered regularization protocol:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Early Stopping:} Triggered by F1-Score plateauing to prevent over-training and preserve generalization.
    \item \textbf{Label Smoothing (0.1):} Applied to the loss function to penalize overconfident logit generation and improve the calibration of the final probability scores.
    \item \textbf{Bottleneck Regularization:} Aggressive dropout was enforced across both models to ensure the resulting spatial identifiers remain robust to the high-entropy noise of real-world address strings.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Generative Synthesis: The Pienza Manifold}
To overcome the constraints of data sparsity ($N \approx 4,700$), the project deployed a \textbf{Conditional Generative Adversarial Network (cGAN)} to synthesize a 1,000,000-row data manifold. Unlike standard generative models that learn a broad distribution, the \textbf{conditional architecture} allows the system to synthesize offers targeted at specific market states. By conditioning the model on attributes—such as \texttt{hour\_of\_day}, \texttt{product\_tier}, and \texttt{zone\_id}—the system can programmatically request synthetic trips for any desired operational scenario.

This implementation effectively transforms the model into an \textbf{Infinite Keras Generator}. Because the neural network functions as a continuous mathematical mapping, it is capable of streaming an unlimited volume of synthetic offers on-demand. While the generator provides this infinite capacity, a fixed 1,000,000-row data manifold was materialized as a static Parquet asset. 

Reaching convergence required seven iterations of feature selection and spatial tuning. To prevent \textbf{Mode Collapse} (a state where the model outputs repetitive, low-variance data), the trip architecture was partitioned into two orthogonal vectors: \textit{Context} and \textit{Physics}.

Initial attempts utilized Uber's H3 hexagonal grid at Resolution 6 ($\approx 36$ km\textsuperscript{2}) for pickup origins. This coarse resolution proved insufficient, conflating distinct strategic sectors. Conversely, shifting to a hyper-granular Resolution 9 was a recipe for overfitting, forcing the model to memorize specific coordinates rather than learning urban distributions. 

This was resolved by executing a \textbf{Point-in-Polygon (PiP)} spatial join. By mapping raw GPS pickups directly to the 42 semantic macro-zones developed in Phase 4, the system unified the spatial axis. This allowed the GAN to learn origin-destination economics without the noise of an abstract grid.

While the ideal model would replicate transient incentives (\textit{e.g., Surge, Turbo+}), these high-variance signals derailed the learning of baseline prices, as these features collapsed to zero after training. To prevent mode collapse and stabilize the Generator, incentives were excised from the feature space; the remaining variables were split into two tensors:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Context Switches (Conditioning):} The state of the world prior to the request (\textit{e.g., Hour of Day, Day of Week, Product Tier, Pickup/Dropoff Zone}).
    \item \textbf{Pure Physics (Generated):} The continuous variables the model must hallucinate (\textit{e.g., Upfront Fare, Estimated Duration, Trip Distance, Pickup Details}).
\end{itemize}

To stabilize the model's activations and ensure realistic outputs, three constraints were applied:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Log-Transformations:} Applied to heavily skewed features (\textit{Fare, Distance}) to stabilize gradients during backpropagation.
    \item \textbf{MinMax Scaling $[-1, 1]$:} Compresses all physical features uniformly to prevent high-magnitude metrics (\textit{e.g., seconds}) from mathematically overwhelming smaller scalars (\textit{e.g., km}).
    \item \textbf{Oversampling:} \textit{Premium} tier trips represent a minuscule fraction of the raw data. To prevent tier blindness, a $15 \times$ oversampling multiplier was applied exclusively to this category, forcing the network to assimilate high-value, low-frequency market behaviors.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Neural Architecture: The Generator Pipeline}
The Generator is architected as a conditional dense network designed to transform real observed data into synthetic,  operationally-valid, ride offers. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:generator_arch}, the model employs a four-stage funnel to synthesize market physics from categorical constraints.



\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_generator_v7.jpg} 
    \caption{Technical Architecture of the Pienza Generator v7.}
    \label{fig:generator_arch}
\end{figure}

The Pienza Generator (v7) utilizes an embedding-concatenation strategy to map market constraints to physical outcomes. The six categorical variables at the input stage (\textit{e.g., temporal blocks, product tier, geospatial zones}) act as \textbf{conditional switches} rather than synthesized outputs. When the system is prompted with a specific market context---such as an 8:00 AM mission on a Monday from Zone A to Zone B---the model's 2.7 million parameters synthesize the exact continuous physics (fare, durations, and distances) that correspond to those specific constraints.

To ensure stochastic variety and prevent repetitive outputs, 100 dimensions of Gaussian noise are fused with the embedded context vectors. This 133-dimensional input is then expanded through a series of three progressive \textbf{Dense Blocks} (ranging from 512 to 2048 neurons). Each block utilizes \textit{Batch Normalization} and \textit{LeakyReLU} activations to maintain mathematical stability during the adversarial training process. The architecture terminates in a five-neuron \textbf{\textit{tanh} output layer}, which generates the normalized values for the core physics metrics. These are subsequently mapped back to real-world units via the inverse transformation of the data scaling protocol.

\noindent \textit{Outcome: This architecture allows for the on-demand generation of an infinite stream of statistically valid ride offers, providing the high-volume data manifold required for the subsequent simulation and deep learning phases.}

\subsubsection{Discriminator Architecture: The Adversarial Validator}
The Discriminator (v7) serves as the adversarial validator within the cGAN framework. Its primary objective is to perform a binary classification task, distinguishing between ground-truth operational records and synthetic hallucinations. To provide the Generator with effective gradient pressure, the Discriminator is architected to evaluate both the physical magnitude of the offer and its environmental context.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{discri2.jpg} 
    \caption{Architecture of the Pienza Discriminator v7.}
    \label{fig:discriminator_arch}
\end{figure}

The architecture utilizes a three-stage validation logic to render its verdict:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Contextual Fusion:} The network ingests the six switches (\textit{e.g., temporal blocks, zone IDs}) through embedding layers. These categorical vectors are flattened and concatenated with the 5-dimensional physics input. This creates a unified 38-dimensional feature vector, ensuring the model evaluates the validity of the fare and duration \textbf{conditional} upon the specific market state.
    
    \item \textbf{Regularized Funnel:} The concatenated signal is processed through a descending dense stack of 1024, 512, and 256 neurons. To prevent the Discriminator from reaching premature convergence---which would stall the Generator's learning---the architecture implements aggressive \textbf{Dropout} layers ($p=0.2$) following each \textit{LeakyReLU} activation. This forces the model to prioritize robust statistical patterns over specific coordinate memorization.
    
    \item \textbf{The Verdict:} The network terminates in a single-neuron output layer. This head utilizes a sigmoid activation to produce a probability score representing the model's confidence in the sample's authenticity.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textit{Outcome: With approximately 697,000 trainable parameters, the v7 Discriminator provides the necessary logic to identify subtle non-linear discrepancies in pricing physics. This adversarial tension ensures that the infinite stream generated by the system maintains 1:1 statistical parity with the original Mexico City operational environment.}


\subsubsection{Training Strategy and Optimization}
Training was executed via synchronized Adam optimizers ($LR=0.0002, \beta_1=0.5$) using a $1.0$ gradient clipping threshold to ensure numerical stability. To prevent the Discriminator from prematurely dominating the adversarial process, a $0.1$ label smoothing factor is applied to the Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss function. The training loop is compiled as a \texttt{@tf.function} graph with a batch size of 64, allowing for efficient backpropagation across the physical and categorical tensors while preserving the statistical correlations of the original marketplace. The architecture was trained over a 1,000-epoch cycle to establish the baseline physical manifold. As illustrated in the loss curves (Figure \ref{fig:gan_convergence}), the adversarial process reached a stable equilibrium within 19.11 minutes.

\subsubsection{Relational Integrity: Correlation Delta Analysis}
Beyond matching individual feature distributions, the generator must replicate the complex interactions between variables---the "Physics" of the marketplace. To quantify this, the project calculated a \textbf{Correlation Delta Matrix} ($\Delta R$). This diagnostic is defined as the element-wise subtraction of the real-world Pearson correlation matrix from the synthetic matrix:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta R = R_{synthetic} - R_{real}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Network Graph Analysis: A Sandbox for Autonomous Vehicles}
The final technical module of the Pienza framework transitions from retrospective row-wise analysis to forward-looking graph-based intelligence. In this stage, the network is no longer conceptualized as a record of human labor, but as a \textbf{high-fidelity sandbox for Autonomous Vehicle (AV) fleet prototyping}. By treating Pienza as a strictly \textbf{geofenced sovereign ecosystem}, the study evaluates the viability of autonomous agents operating within a closed-loop urban environment.

To ensure the stability of the stochastic optimization, both models are architected as \textbf{Closed Systems}:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{The Topological Geofence Blueprint (Undirected):} This model defines the physical constraints of the AV operation. It transforms the 72 polygons into a schematic grid where edges represent the only allowed traversal paths for a self-governed fleet. By utilizing Sigma and Omega coefficients, the study identifies the "Small-World" efficiency of the geofence, marking critical infrastructure bottlenecks (\textit{e.g., node\_reforma\_palmas}) and identifying single-point-of-failure cut-vertices that would compromise fleet connectivity.
    
    \item \textbf{The Mobility Tensor (Directed/Steady-State):} To model the thermodynamics of capital, the study performs a \textbf{Topological Pruning} of the 1,000,000-row synthetic manifold. All offers originating from or destined for the "exterior" (the Exilio) are dropped, isolating the \textbf{Internal Pienza Flow (IPF)}. This results in a refined dataset of \textbf{461,003 observations (45.6\% of the manifold)}, representing a pure, high-density mobility state. This directed graph maps the flow of value, revealing an autocentric core with a diameter of 2, proving that within the geofence, the AV agent is always within two economic transitions of a peak-performance node.
\end{itemize}

The network analysis establishes the \textbf{Bridge to Markov} for autonomous navigation by applying the \textit{Bellman Equation} to the internal mobility tensor. By calculating the \textbf{State-Value Function} ($V(s)$), the framework transforms the geofenced urban grid into a series of interconnected decision nodes. This enables the derivation of a prescriptive $Q$-Matrix policy, allowing a prototype autonomous fleet to execute optimized five-jump mission sequences that prioritize long-term economic sovereignty over myopic fare-chasing. 

However, it must be explicitly noted that a \textbf{Stationary MDP} is a static approximation of a non-linear, time-variant system. While this model provides a robust steady-state baseline for strategic positioning, it remains a necessary oversimplification of the high-frequency volatility that a full-scale \textbf{Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)} simulation or a dynamic \textbf{Reinforcement Learning (RL)} agent would encounter. Consequently, the \textbf{Generative Moonshot} phase concludes here: it does not attempt to execute dynamic learning or stochastic sampling. Instead, it successfully delivers the \textbf{functional scaffolding}---mathematically defining the state space ($\mathcal{S}$), transition probabilities ($\mathcal{P}$), and reward manifolds ($\mathcal{R}$)---required to transition from descriptive science to autonomous execution in Pienza 2.0: The Knowledge.

\subsection{Stochastic Scaffolding: The Bridge to Markov}

To transition from market observation to autonomous optimization, the Mobility Tensor must be converted into a mathematical environment compatible with \textbf{Dynamic Programming}. This stage establishes the \textbf{Markov Decision Process (MDP)} scaffolding, enabling an agent to evaluate not just the immediate value of a mission, but the long-term potential of the resulting urban positioning.

The transition is operationalized by extracting two fundamental matrices from the Internal Pienza Flow (IPF) manifold: the \textbf{Transition Matrix ($\mathcal{P}$)} and the \textbf{Reward Matrix ($\mathcal{R}$)}.

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep, topsep=3pt]
    \item \textbf{Transition Matrix ($P_{ij}$):} This matrix represents the "behavioral gravity" of the marketplace. For every zone $i$, the model calculates the empirical probability that the next assigned mission will terminate in zone $j$. This captures the stochastic nature of the algorithm: if an agent is in \textit{Anzures}, what is the statistical likelihood that the market will "propel" them toward \textit{Santa Fe} versus \textit{Polanco}?
    
    \item \textbf{Reward Matrix ($R_{ij}$):} This defines the expected instantaneous payoff for each jump. It records the weighted average EPH (Earnings Per Hour) for a mission starting in $i$ and ending in $j$. This matrix acts as the economic fuel of the system, quantifying the immediate return of any specific movement.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Structural Stability: Absorbing States and Total Retention}

A critical requirement for Markovian stability is that the transition matrix must be \textbf{row-stochastic}---meaning the probabilities for every origin node must sum exactly to 1.0 to ensure that no "probability mass" is lost during the simulation. During the construction phase, the framework identified four \textbf{Absorbing States} within the 72-node geofence: \textit{bosque\_1, campo\_marte, lomas\_olimpo,} and \textit{santa\_fe\_bosques\_de}.

To preserve mathematical integrity and prevent the simulation from collapsing into a null state, these nodes are assigned a self-loop probability of $P_{ii} = 1.0$. 

Strategically, these are identified as \textbf{Terminal Retention Nodes}. They represent zones where the agent captures high-value arrivals but finds zero market liquidity to exit the area.
